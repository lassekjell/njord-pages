1. Executive Summary

Two motion recordings from Northern Gale reveal that the vessel experiences significant roll motion even in moderate sea conditions, with performance degrading rapidly as sea state increases. In the mild sea state (Bft 3–4), roll reaches +/-6.5 degrees — already at the upper limit of comfortable survey operations. In the rough sea state, roll doubles to +/-12.9 degrees with lateral accelerations entering the ISO 2631-1 "a little uncomfortable" to "fairly uncomfortable" range at exposed crew positions.

Pitch, which is normally well-controlled on a catamaran, increases by ~4.7x in the rough conditions (range 3.1° to 14.6°), indicating exposure to head/following seas during parts of the recording. Note: Log 1 pitch has a +1.07° mean trim — raw range is -0.38° to +2.71°, de-trended amplitude ±1.6°. Roll rates peak at +/-20 deg/s — fast, snappy motions that drive crew fatigue and seasickness far more than equivalent amplitudes at slower periods.

This report documents the measured motion characteristics as a baseline for evaluating operational limits and potential motion mitigation measures.

8. Observations and Findings

Roll is the dominant motion in both sea states. Peak roll is consistently the largest angular displacement. The catamaran hull form is inherently stiff in pitch but lively in roll — this is a structural characteristic of the vessel type.
At Bft 3–4 (mild), the vessel already reaches +/-6.5 deg roll. This is the lower bound of typical North Sea operational conditions. Roll of +/-12.9 deg in rougher conditions indicates serious motion challenges during winter operations.
Lateral accelerations enter ISO 2631-1 "a little uncomfortable" range at main deck and wheelhouse. With RMS values of 0.049–0.060g (unweighted), the wheelhouse exceeds the Swedish AFS action value (0.051g). Peak accelerations reach 0.30g at the wheelhouse. Mid-height positions (3–5 m) benefit from proximity to the roll centre.
Pitch range increases ~4.7x between sea states. The heading data shows the vessel traversed all headings (0–360 deg) during the log, exposing the vessel to head and following seas. Pitch rate peaks of +/-13 deg/s indicate slamming risk on the catamaran cross-structure.
Roll period is fast (approx 4 s in mild conditions). Short roll periods produce rapid, snappy motions that are physiologically more nauseogenic than equivalent amplitudes at longer periods. This is driven by the catamaran's high metacentric height (GM).
Heave triples from +/-0.45m to +/-1.36m. For survey operations using multibeam or other depth-sensitive equipment, this level of heave motion requires robust heave compensation performance from the Seapath 320.
Data quality is excellent throughout both recordings. All samples report Normal status across attitude, heading, heave, and velocity. RTK GPS with 12 satellites and HDOP 0.7–0.8. The data is survey-grade and suitable for engineering analysis.

9. Conclusions

Northern Gale exhibits motion characteristics typical of a catamaran survey vessel: good pitch stability in moderate conditions, but rapid, high-amplitude roll that degrades significantly with increasing sea state. The measured data shows:

Roll is the primary operational limitation. At +/-12.9 deg in rough conditions, this affects crew comfort, equipment accuracy, and the vessel's operational weather window.
Crew comfort is compromised in rough conditions. ISO 2631-1 assessment (unweighted) shows "a little uncomfortable" conditions at main deck (1 m) and wheelhouse (8 m) positions. The wheelhouse lateral RMS of 0.060g (0.59 m/s²) exceeds the Swedish AFS action value. Prolonged exposure at these levels drives fatigue.
The vessel's weather window is limited. If +/-6.5 deg roll at Bft 3–4 is near the limit for precision survey work, the number of available operational days in North Sea conditions is significantly constrained.

This report establishes the measured motion baseline for Northern Gale. It is recommended as input for evaluating motion mitigation measures and defining operational limits for survey work.
